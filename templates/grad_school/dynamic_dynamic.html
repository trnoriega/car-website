Dynamic ribosomes and dynamic signal recognition particles

Previously, only signal recognition particles (SRPs) interacting with ribosomes that had been forced into a static conformation could be studied. This meant that we could not learn about a whole set of the interaction’s dynamics.

By using a modified microscopic setup, originally designed to sequence DNA, I was able to observe ribosomes undergoing their normal dynamics at the same time as they interacted with SRPs.

In this setup 150,000 holes, each with a 1-micron diameter, are patterned into a thin slice of aluminum. Such a slice is shown below. The dark circles in the zoomed in inset on the lower right of the image (expanded from the region surrounded by the dashed rectangle) are the microscopic holes. Each one of these holes serves as a tiny reaction volume were dynamic ribosomes can be observed interacting with dynamic SRPs, thereby recapitulating the complete interaction.

I converted each of the reactions occurring in the microscopic holes into data-sets, so that the dynamics of SRP-ribosome interactions could be analyzed. I wrote scripts that allowed me to narrow down which wells contained useful information as well as carefully annotate the useful signals. For example, the image below shows a single ribosome that is changing over time (represented by the green and orange lines) interacting with multiple SRPs (represented by the red line). The nature of each signal is indicated by the arrows and circles.

By analyzing thousands of these dynamic SRP-ribosome interactions I have been able to confirm past observations. For example, the duration of SRP-ribosome interactions depends on how many times each ribosome has previously interacted with SRP (as shown in the box plot below).

More importantly, I have also been able to gain significant new insights into the biology of how SRP interacts with ribosomes. For example, because I can track ribosome dynamics, I was able to observe exactly when SRP starts interacting with ribosomes. The image below shows that SRP’s first interactions with a ribosome mostly occur at a very specific ribosome state.

The combination of a coordinated first arrival with increasingly long interactions leads to a switch-like behavior of SRP-ribosome interactions. Ribosomes only really interact with SRP after they change into a particular state, as shown in the figure below. This helps explain a long-standing question about how SRPs in the cell avoid non-productive interactions with ribosomes.
